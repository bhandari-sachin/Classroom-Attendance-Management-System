## Part 2: Project Reflection

### How the Project Evolved

The project began with a clear but broad ambition — to build a Classroom Attendance Management System using agile principles across an eight-sprint academic semester. In the early sprints, the team focused on laying the groundwork: forming roles, selecting tools, and establishing the technical skeleton. What started as separate backend and frontend components gradually came together into a cohesive, integrated system. By Sprint 4, the application was demonstrable end-to-end, with QR-based attendance, role-based dashboards, and Docker deployment all functioning. The later sprints shifted focus from building to refining — improving code quality with SonarQube, adding multilingual support, and validating the system through rigorous testing.

### What the Team Learned

One of the clearest lessons across the sprints was the cost of deferred integration. In Sprint 2, the frontend and backend were built in parallel but not connected, which created debugging overhead in Sprint 3. Similarly, testing and CI/CD setup, when left to later sprints, added pressure that could have been reduced by embedding these practices earlier. The team grew more deliberate about these decisions over time, and by the final sprints, the workflow — committing to GitHub, running Jenkins pipelines, checking SonarQube — had become routine rather than reactive.

### How Agile and Scrum Worked in Practice

Agile provided a useful rhythm for an academic project where requirements evolved and team availability fluctuated. The two-week sprint cadence aligned well with the course schedule, and Trello and GitHub served as effective collaboration tools for a part-time, distributed team. Sprint reviews created natural checkpoints that kept the team accountable and gave a clear sense of progress. That said, some Scrum practices were lighter in execution than in theory — retrospectives were informal, and velocity was not formally tracked. Despite this, the iterative approach meant the team could course-correct sprint by sprint rather than discovering problems at the end.

### Final Reflection

Over eight sprints, the team delivered a fully functional, tested, and documented Classroom Attendance Management System from scratch. The project demonstrated that agile methods can work effectively in an academic setting when the team commits to consistent communication, incremental delivery, and honest retrospection. The most significant growth was not just in the system itself, but in the team's understanding of what it means to build software collaboratively — navigating integration challenges, maintaining code quality under time pressure, and ultimately delivering something that meets real user needs.
